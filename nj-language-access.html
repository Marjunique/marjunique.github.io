U.S. Digital Response for NJ Department of Labor | August 2025

Language Access for NJ Temporary Disability + Family Leave Programs

Developing AI-powered translation systems with validated multilingual glossaries to enhance communication for Spanish and Haitian Creole-speaking communities accessing New Jersey's TDI/FLI benefits.

My Contribution

As Project Manager and UX Researcher, I led the end-to-end development of an AI-powered translation system for the NJ Department of Labor's Temporary Disability and Family Leave programs.

•

Led mixed-methods research including 1:1 interviews with 5 Spanish-speaking participants and group interviews with Haitian Creole speakers to validate translation quality

•

Developed and refined 20+ Haitian Creole glossary terms and plain language guidelines for English content

•

Collaborated with community-based organizations and NJDOL staff to ensure cultural and linguistic accuracy

•

Created AI translation prompts and evaluation rubrics that resulted in translations outperforming vendor quality

Project Goal

Support the NJ Department of Labor in developing AI-powered translations that ensure consistency, use program-specific terminology, and provide clear translations for Spanish and Haitian Creole-speaking communities accessing TDI/FLI benefits.

Current Challenges

  • • No standardized bilingual glossary of terms for TDI and FLI programs
  • • Complex terms are difficult to explain clearly, especially in translation
  • • Spanish translations lag weeks behind English content updates
  • • Haitian Creole translations are inconsistent, with community members perceiving quality variably

Intended Outcomes

  • • Validated English, Spanish, and Haitian Creole glossary for TDI/FLI programs
  • • Translation prompt library and evaluation rubrics to assess AI outputs
  • • Usage guidelines for scaling translations
  • • Framework for expanding to additional languages

Process & Methods

Phase 1: Plain English Glossary Development

Methods: Content analysis, plain language evaluation using PlainLanguage.gov checklist

Updated existing glossary to meet accessibility standards, ensuring 6th-8th grade reading level, active voice, and contextualized definitions within the user journey of qualifying for benefits.

Key Activities:

• Reviewed 40+ technical terms and rewrote for clarity
• Applied plain language principles: direct address, short sentences, common words
• Added contextual information about deadlines and eligibility criteria

Phase 2: AI Translation Prompt Development

Methods: Iterative prompt engineering, stakeholder feedback sessions

Developed language-specific prompts that incorporated translation guidelines, cultural nuances, and program-specific terminology. Refined prompts based on feedback from community organizations and NJDOL staff.

Prompt Criteria Included:

• Accuracy and relevance (maintaining government terminology)
• Cultural sensitivity (gender neutrality for Spanish, minimizing French interference for Haitian Creole)
• Consistency in tone and formatting
• Accessibility aligned with W3C standards

Phase 3: Community Testing & Validation

Methods: 1:1 comparative interviews, focus groups, translation quality assessment

Spanish Testing (n=5 participants)

Conducted 1:1 interviews comparing AI-generated vs human translations of 12 glossary definitions

• Participants reviewed randomized comparison decks to reduce bias
• Rated difficulty of understanding on 1-5 scale
• Provided preference ratings and qualitative feedback

Haitian Creole Testing (group sessions)

Conducted group interviews comparing Google Translate vs AI vs human translations of 8 glossary definitions

• Participants ranked three versions (A, B, C) for each term
• Discussed contextual appropriateness and cultural relevance
• Identified terms with strong legal or formal connotations

Findings

Spanish Translation Quality

Key Insight

AI-produced translations reached comparable comprehension levels to human translations, with no significant difference in difficulty ratings. Most participants showed no strong preference between versions.

Additional Findings:

• Participants confused by technical terms and acronyms in both versions

• Preferred conversational language and organized formatting

• When preferences existed, human translations selected twice as often as AI

Haitian Creole Translation Quality

Clear AI preference

AI-generated translations ranked #1 most frequently across 8 terms tested

Google Translate: Often too literal or formal

AI-generated: Best balance of accuracy and conversational tone

Human translations: Mixed preferences, sometimes too formal

Key Insight

Participants strongly preferred AI translations that maintained conversational Haitian Creole while providing contextual explanations for technical terms like "claim" and "leave."

Additional Findings:

• Words like "claim" carried strong legal weight requiring explanation

• Overly formal translations created distance from community members

• Contextual definitions significantly improved comprehension
