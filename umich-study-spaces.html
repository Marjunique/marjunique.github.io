University of Michigan Searchable/Filterable Study Spaces

University of Michigan Library — October 2022 - April 2023

Role

UX Researcher

Timeline

7 months (Oct 2022 - Apr 2023)

Methods

Competitive analysis, Usability testing, Data analysis

The Challenge

How can we improve our study space reservation system with searchable/filterable features to make our spaces findable?

The Library Environments UX Research Team and the Library Information Technology Design and Discovery Team collaborated to improve the user experience of the Library's study spaces booking website. Students experienced issues searching and booking studying spaces on the library's booking site, often unable to find spaces that matched their needs for noise level, atmosphere, and amenities.

Research Approach

I led the usability testing phase of this collaborative research project, conducting tests across multiple university booking systems to understand how students interact with different interface patterns and filter mechanisms.

Research Activities

  • • Competitive analysis of 4 university booking systems (Cambridge, NYU, UT Austin, Duke)
  • • 15 usability tests with U of M students
  • • Analysis of filter categories, functionality, and search-ability
  • • Mock-up testing of vertical and horizontal filter layouts
  • • Synthesis of qualitative feedback themes

Research Process

1. Competitive Analysis

We analyzed booking systems at various universities to compare filter categories, filter functionality, search-ability, atmosphere descriptions, location metadata, and features. We selected University of Cambridge, NYU, UT Austin, and Duke as our databases for usability testing, as they closely matched our design mockups.

2. Mock-up Development

The UX designer created two mockups of the study space booking website with filter values for "Noise level" and "Atmosphere" as well as views of filter components in use. We tested both vertical and horizontal versions to understand which layout students preferred.

3. Usability Testing

I conducted 15 usability tests, carefully optimizing task order and combinations to prevent bias and data manipulation. Tests were conducted across multiple university booking systems to understand how students interact with different interface patterns and what features they found most helpful.

4. Data Analysis

We identified key themes and highlighted outliers across all testing sessions. This analysis supported our design decisions for the future reservation booking system and provided clear direction for feature prioritization.

Key Findings

We learned that students want a website that is easy to navigate, yet provides the necessary information to book a suitable study space. Their needs went beyond basic filtering—they wanted context, guidance, and visual confirmation.

1. Visual Information is Critical

Students emphasized the importance of images and descriptions to understand space characteristics before booking. They wanted to see what the space looked like and understand its atmosphere, not just know its technical specifications.

2. Students Need Navigation Support

Students needed directions and guided instructions throughout the booking process. The current systems assumed familiarity with library spaces and booking procedures that new users didn't have.

3. Filtering Out Non-Reservable Spaces

Students were frustrated when they found spaces that couldn't actually be reserved. They were eager for a feature that would filter out non-reservable spaces from results, saving time and reducing confusion.

4. Noise Level Indicators Highly Requested

Indicators for noise levels were highly requested to help students find appropriate study environments. Students noted that noise level was often their primary decision factor when choosing a space.

Recommendations

1. Implement Robust Filtering System

Add filters for noise level, atmosphere, group size, and amenities, with the ability to filter out non-reservable spaces.

2. Add Visual Context to Space Listings

Include photos and detailed descriptions for each space so students can make informed decisions before booking.

3. Provide Guided Navigation

Add tooltips, help text, and step-by-step instructions for first-time users navigating the booking system.

4. Standardize Space Metadata

Ensure all spaces have consistent information about noise levels, atmosphere, capacity, and available amenities.

Reflection

This research process taught me how to apply service design principles to my research strategy. Throughout the process, I learned that centering our users throughout the process aligned the most with our goals.

Usability testing provided significant insight on where users experienced challenges which resulted in me developing more empathy for our users. Watching students struggle with interfaces I thought were intuitive was humbling and reminded me why direct user research is irreplaceable.

I also learned to adopt flexibility during usability testing. Sometimes you'll encounter users who are short with responses, so you have to adapt and ask questions in different ways to better assist the user and gather meaningful insights.
