Improving Daycare Access Through Journey Mapping

U.S. Digital Response for Miami Department of Human Services — 2024

Role

Lead UX Researcher

Timeline

9 weeks (Aug - Oct 2024)

Methods

Journey mapping, Stakeholder interviews, Service design

Project Goal

Create a comprehensive journey map of Miami's daycare application process to identify pain points and opportunities for wraparound services—particularly for English, Spanish, and Haitian Creole-speaking communities.

Current Challenges

  • • Process never formally researched or documented
  • • Extensive waitlists with no visibility
  • • Mix of paper and digital processes
  • • Unclear barriers for families

Intended Outcomes

  • • Map complete parent journey
  • • Identify actionable pain points
  • • Recommend wraparound services
  • • Improve language access

My Contribution

As Lead UX Researcher, I led a 9-week mixed-methods research engagement, conducting interviews with parents, daycare staff, and administrators to map the entire application journey from discovery to enrollment.

  • • Conducted interviews with 15+ parents, staff, and administrators
  • • Mapped end-to-end user journeys across 5 distinct stages
  • • Performed document analysis of 12+ application forms and materials
  • • Evaluated language access for Spanish and Haitian Creole speakers
  • • Synthesized findings into actionable recommendations

Phase 1: Discovering Daycare Service

Methods: Stakeholder interviews, document review

Interviewed parents about how they first learned about Miami's daycare programs and what information was available online or in-person. Most discovered the service through word-of-mouth rather than official channels.

Key Activities:

  • • Interviewed parents at discovery stage about information channels
  • • Reviewed city website and signage for daycare visibility
  • • Documented how Spanish and Haitian Creole speakers found information

Phase 2: Filling out 1st Application

Methods: Form analysis, parent interviews

Analyzed 12+ application forms and interviewed parents about completing initial paperwork. Identified redundant information requests and language access challenges for non-English speakers.

Key Activities:

  • • Catalogued all required fields across application forms
  • • Identified redundant information (e.g., emergency contacts repeated multiple times)
  • • Assessed availability and quality of Spanish and Haitian Creole translations

Phase 3: Waitlist Period

Methods: Parent interviews, staff interviews

Explored how parents managed uncertainty during waitlist periods, which could last up to 9 months. Documented what communication they received (or didn't receive) from the department and their stress levels.

Key Activities:

  • • Interviewed parents about waitlist visibility and communication
  • • Documented frequency of check-in calls from anxious parents
  • • Identified gaps in status updates and position transparency

Phase 4: Filling out 2nd Application

Methods: Parent interviews, timeline analysis

Documented the second application process and extremely short time constraints parents faced when called from the waitlist—sometimes just 3 days to submit all documentation.

Key Activities:

  • • Mapped turnaround time requirements from call to submission
  • • Identified documentation that parents struggled to obtain quickly
  • • Documented consequences of missing deadlines (lost spots)

Phase 5: Receive Decision of Acceptance

Methods: Parent interviews, service design analysis

Examined how families received and responded to acceptance decisions and what support was available during enrollment. Identified opportunities for wraparound services.

Key Activities:

  • • Interviewed families about acceptance notification process
  • • Documented what happened after acceptance (next steps, support)
  • • Identified opportunities for additional family services

Findings

16+

Pain Points Identified

Across 5 journey stages affecting families, particularly those facing language and documentation barriers

1

Discovery Relies on Word of Mouth

Most parents learned about the daycare from friends or by chance—not through city websites or signage. Online information was minimal and facilities were difficult to locate.

"I found the daycare by walking by the library next door. If I was driving, I wouldn't have noticed it."

2

Redundant Paperwork Creates Burden

Parents appreciated the application's clarity but were frustrated by repetitive information requests, especially emergency contacts appearing across multiple forms.

"It's frustrating to write the same emergency contact information over and over again. Why can't they just ask once?"

3

Language Barriers Persist Despite Translation

Parents with limited English proficiency relied on Google Translate or family members. Translated documents alone weren't sufficient—interpreters were needed for complex processes.

"They should have an interpreter at the office to help Haitian Creole speakers. Not just a form in Creole—actual help."

4

Waitlist Opacity Causes Anxiety

Parents had no visibility into their waitlist position, leading to stress and frequent check-in calls. Some waited up to 9 months with no status updates.

"The waitlist is a black hole. I called every month just to make sure they didn't forget about me."

5

Short Turnaround Creates Documentation Stress

Once called from the waitlist, parents had extremely short deadlines to submit additional paperwork—sometimes just 3 days—risking lost spots if documents weren't ready.

"The turnaround time was so short. They called me on Friday, and I had to submit everything by Monday. It was stressful."

Impact

The journey map serves as a foundational tool for Miami's Department of Human Services to improve parent experience and operational efficiency, guiding ongoing process improvements and preparing for CiviForm integration.

Key Outcomes

  • • Delivered comprehensive journey map with actionable recommendations for digitization, language support, and waitlist transparency
  • • Provided blueprint for making daycare services more accessible to Miami's diverse communities
  • • Prepared department for future integration of CiviForm platform for streamlining public benefits applications
  • • Established framework for ongoing process improvements based on user feedback
