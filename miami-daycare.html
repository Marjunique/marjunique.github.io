Improving Daycare Access Through Journey Mapping

U.S. Digital Response for Miami Department of Human Services — 2024

Role

Lead UX Researcher

Timeline

9 weeks (Aug - Oct 2024)

Methods

Journey mapping, Stakeholder interviews, Service design

The Challenge

Miami's Department of Human Services manages daycare programs serving families across the city, but the application process had never been formally researched or documented. With extensive waitlists and a mix of paper and digital processes, families faced unclear barriers to accessing critical childcare services.

The project goal was to create a comprehensive journey map identifying pain points and opportunities for wraparound services—particularly for English, Spanish, and Haitian Creole-speaking communities.

Research Approach

I led a 9-week mixed-methods research engagement, conducting interviews with parents, daycare staff, and administrators to map the entire application journey from discovery to enrollment.

Research Activities

  • • Interviews with 15+ parents, staff, and administrators
  • • Stakeholder interviews across Miami Department of Human Services
  • • Document analysis of 12+ application forms and materials
  • • Process mapping across 5 distinct journey stages
  • • Language access evaluation for Spanish and Haitian Creole speakers

Key Findings

1. Discovery Relies on Word of Mouth

Most parents learned about the daycare from friends or by chance—not through city websites or signage. Online information was minimal and facilities were difficult to locate.

"I found the daycare by walking by the library next door. If I was driving, I wouldn't have noticed it."

2. Redundant Paperwork Creates Burden

Parents appreciated the application's clarity but were frustrated by repetitive information requests, especially emergency contacts appearing across multiple forms.

"It's frustrating to write the same emergency contact information over and over again. Why can't they just ask once?"

3. Language Barriers Persist Despite Translation

Parents with limited English proficiency relied on Google Translate or family members. Translated documents alone weren't sufficient—interpreters were needed for complex processes.

"They should have an interpreter at the office to help Haitian Creole speakers. Not just a form in Creole—actual help."

4. Waitlist Opacity Causes Anxiety

Parents had no visibility into their waitlist position, leading to stress and frequent check-in calls. Some waited up to 9 months with no status updates.

"The waitlist is a black hole. I called every month just to make sure they didn't forget about me."

5. Short Turnaround Creates Documentation Stress

Once called from the waitlist, parents had extremely short deadlines to submit additional paperwork—sometimes just 3 days—risking lost spots if documents weren't ready.

"The turnaround time was so short. They called me on Friday, and I had to submit everything by Monday. It was stressful."

Journey Map

The comprehensive journey map documented five stages of the parent experience: discovering daycare services, filling out the first application, waitlist period, second application submission, and receiving acceptance. Each stage identified what's working, what's not working, opportunities for improvement, language access barriers, and success metrics.

Top Recommendations

1. Improve Visibility & Access to Information

Enhance signage at daycare locations, improve the City of Miami website with clear information, and increase community outreach through social media and physical flyers.

2. Digitize & Streamline the Application Process

Introduce an online application system where parents can fill out and submit forms, upload required documents, and check their application status.

3. Reduce Redundancy in Paperwork

Consolidate emergency contact fields and other repetitive information to make applications more efficient.

4. Provide Language Support Beyond Translations

Offer interpreter assistance in-person or via phone/video for non-English speakers instead of relying solely on translated documents.

5. Increase Transparency in the Waitlist Process

Implement an online waitlist tracker where parents can see their position in line, estimated wait times, and any required next steps.

Impact & Next Steps

The journey map serves as a foundational tool for Miami's Department of Human Services to improve the parent experience and operational efficiency. It will guide ongoing process improvements and prepare the department for future integration of CiviForm, an open-source platform for streamlining public benefits applications.

By identifying 16+ pain points across the journey and providing actionable recommendations, this research creates a blueprint for making daycare services more accessible to Miami's diverse communities—particularly families facing language and documentation barriers.

Reflection

This project reinforced how critical it is to map the full user journey, not just the "official" touchpoints. Parents' experiences began long before they found the application form—with chance encounters, word-of-mouth recommendations, and searches for invisible facilities.

The most valuable insight was understanding that language access isn't just about translation—it's about providing human support at every step. Haitian Creole and Spanish-speaking parents needed interpreters who could guide them through the process, not just documents in their language.
