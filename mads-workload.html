Master of Applied Data Science Workload Investigation

University of Michigan — August - December 2022

Role

UX Research Consultant

Timeline

5 months (Aug - Dec 2022)

Methods

Contextual inquiry, Stakeholder interviews, Data analysis

The Challenge

What strategies can be implemented to effectively address workload data collection issues and offer actionable recommendations for improvement?

The Master of Applied Data Science (MADS) program at University of Michigan needed to better understand how course workload data was being collected, processed, and used across different departments. The EyesOnYou research team was tasked with investigating the end-to-end process to identify gaps and improve data collection for better course planning and student experience.

Research Approach

As part of the EyesOnYou Team, I conducted a contextual inquiry to understand MADS' workload data collection process, the Course Operations Team, and their connection to instructors. Our goal was to map the entire workflow and identify where breakdowns occurred.

Research Activities

  • • Background research on existing processes and documentation
  • • 6 stakeholder interviews with faculty and staff across departments
  • • Contextual inquiry sessions observing actual workflows
  • • Affinity wall building for pattern identification
  • • Communication flow mapping

Research Process

1. Background Research

My team and I conducted individual background research on the MADS program structure, course operations, and existing data collection methods. We then collaborated to create a comprehensive interview protocol, testing and refining it for gathering insights from various departments.

2. Contextual Inquiry

We conducted 6 interviews with faculty and staff from various departments—including Course Operations, Academic Affairs, and Instructional teams. Following interviews, we were able to understand communication patterns and identify gaps within the process that weren't visible in documentation alone.

3. Data Analysis

We used affinity wall building to synthesize our findings and identify patterns across different departments and roles. This revealed systemic issues that weren't obvious from individual interviews but became clear when we looked at the data holistically.

Key Findings

We identified three critical issues affecting the workload data collection process, each contributing to incomplete data and frustrated stakeholders.

1. Departmental Transparency Gap

There was a significant lack of transparency between stakeholders about how workload data was being collected, processed, and used. Different departments had incomplete pictures of the overall process. Course Operations didn't know how Academic Affairs used the data, and instructors had no visibility into what happened after they submitted information.

2. Automation Without Follow-up

While systems were automated for instructors to submit workload data, there was insufficient follow-up to ensure instructors understood how to use them or had their questions answered. Automated emails went out, but no one checked if instructors actually completed the forms or needed help understanding what was being asked.

3. Inconsistent Terminology

Different departments used varying terminology for the same concepts, creating confusion and communication barriers in the data collection process. What Course Operations called "workload estimates" was "time investment data" to Academic Affairs and "course hours" to instructors—all referring to the same information.

Communication Flow Analysis

We mapped the current communication flow between stakeholders and identified where transparency was lacking. We then developed an ideal vision of the process that addressed these gaps through clearer handoffs, shared terminology, and regular check-ins.

Current State Issues

  • • One-way communication from Course Operations to instructors
  • • No feedback loop on data completeness or quality
  • • Unclear ownership of follow-up when instructors don't respond
  • • Isolated departmental silos with no shared understanding

Recommendations

We developed a multi-step system to standardize data collection, disbursement, and utilization. The recommendations focus on transparency, communication, regular surveys, and debriefs to create a continuous improvement cycle.

1. Create Shared Data Dictionary

Establish standardized terminology across all departments for workload-related concepts, published in an accessible shared document.

2. Implement Regular Instructor Check-ins

Schedule follow-up meetings after data submission deadlines to answer questions, address confusion, and ensure data quality.

3. Increase Cross-Departmental Visibility

Create quarterly meetings where Course Operations, Academic Affairs, and instructors share how workload data is being used and what insights have been gained.

4. Establish Feedback Loop

Provide instructors with summary reports showing how their workload estimates compared to actual student time investment, closing the loop on data collection.

5. Modular Implementation Approach

Design the system with smaller-scale improvements that can be implemented in different combinations to achieve specific goals, allowing for incremental progress rather than requiring a complete overhaul.

Reflection

Developing recommendations to improve a data collection process taught me that many factors must be taken into account before drawing conclusions on the problem. What appeared to be a simple data collection issue was actually a complex web of communication gaps, unclear ownership, and misaligned expectations.

The interview process revealed that interviewing different participants yields different and overlapping insights. It really highlighted the importance of interview questions and how that can impact your ability to retrieve information. Some stakeholders were very forthcoming about problems, while others required more probing to uncover the same issues from a different angle.
