Phase 1: Communication Research

Methods: Semi-structured interviews, ethnographic observation

Conducted interviews with asylum seekers from diverse backgrounds to understand how they receive and process information. Explored communication customs in home countries and how cultural differences impact trust in government messaging.

Key Activities:

  • • Interviewed migrants about preferred communication channels
  • • Documented cultural differences in information processing (oral vs. visual traditions)
  • • Identified misconceptions about reticketing service (deportation fears, payment confusion)

Phase 2: Frontline Staff Insights

Methods: Stakeholder interviews, site visits

Interviewed frontline employees and translators working directly with asylum seekers to understand communication breakdowns and successful strategies. Learned how staff adapted messaging based on cultural context.

Key Activities:

  • • Interviewed reticketing office coordinators and translators
  • • Documented staff observations about information gaps
  • • Identified need for oral communication alongside visual materials

Phase 3: Information Architecture Development

Methods: Content strategy, multilingual design

Developed information architecture for poster content focusing on clarity, trust-building, and cultural appropriateness. Determined what information was most critical to include and how to structure it across three languages.

Key Activities:

  • • Identified key messages that build trust (voluntary, no-cost, choice)
  • • Structured information hierarchy for visual posters
  • • Ensured messaging addressed common misconceptions directly

Phase 4: Co-Design & Refinement

Methods: Iterative testing, feedback sessions

Tested preliminary poster designs and messaging with asylum seekers and staff. Refined content based on feedback to ensure clarity and cultural sensitivity across all three languages.

Key Activities:

  • • Co-designed poster content with asylum seekers
  • • Tested messaging comprehension across language groups
  • • Refined visual hierarchy and placement strategies

Findings

3

Critical Communication Themes

That shape how asylum seekers receive and trust information

1

Identity and Culture Shape Communication

Cultural backgrounds profoundly influence how asylum seekers receive information. Many African communities prefer oral communication over visual posters, while communication customs, educational backgrounds, and socio-economic factors all impact information processing.

"Most of the guests here are from Africa and in Africa important information is [often] passed orally rather than through posters or visual. We recognized that we needed to talk to the guests."

2

Perceptions and Beliefs Create Barriers

Asylum seekers hold varying misconceptions about the reticketing service. Some perceive it as deportation, others believe it bypasses visa requirements. Rapidly evolving asylum laws make it difficult for staff to provide up-to-date information.

"They think that when you come and get a ticket [through the reticketing service], you're getting deported and that sometimes you have to pay for the ticket."

3

Fragmented Information Sources

Asylum seekers receive information through a patchwork of word-of-mouth, social media (TikTok, WhatsApp), and formal channels. Many don't know reticketing exists despite staff mentioning it at touchpoints, relying instead on informal networks.

"I started going through Google to look for where I can get assistance. One name kept appearing. I went to YouTube, and I saw some of her activities."

4

Trust Must Be Built Through Messaging

Information architecture needed to explicitly address fears and misconceptions. Emphasizing that service is voluntary, no-cost, and provides choice helped build trust. Rebranding from "reticketing" to "reconnections" reduced deportation associations.

Impact

Research informed the design of multilingual communication tools and a comprehensive branding strategy that humanized the service and addressed cultural communication barriers across diverse asylum seeker communities.

Key Outcomes

  • • Designed multilingual posters in English, Spanish, and French informed by cultural communication preferences
  • • Developed information architecture emphasizing trust-building messages (voluntary, no-cost, choice)
  • • Created Reconnections Guidebook training frontline staff on culturally sensitive facilitation
  • • Contributed to rebranding strategy moving away from "reticketing" to reduce deportation associations
  • • Posters piloted in shelters across NYC with plans for citywide scaling
  • • Research identified need for oral communication to complement visual materials, particularly for African communities
