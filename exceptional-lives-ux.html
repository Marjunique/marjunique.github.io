Empathizing with Users through UX Research

Exceptional Lives — August - October 2022

Role

UX Researcher

Timeline

3 months (Aug - Oct 2022)

Methods

User scenarios, Survey design, Workshop facilitation

The Challenge

The people deciding how information was presented on Exceptional Lives' website didn't understand the mental models users were trying to use to navigate it. The website's design was cluttered with too much information on each page and an overall lack of visual hierarchy.

New users found navigating the website confusing, and pages contained long, unformatted text leading to information overload.

Research Objective

Our research aimed to test the taxonomy and foster empathy within the team by encouraging them to put themselves in the shoes of our users—families navigating special education resources.

Research Process

1. Planning

I developed a survey with scenarios and tasks to prompt the team to empathize with users. The scenarios simulated real parent journeys through the website, asking team members to find specific resources as if they were families in need.

2. Distribution

I deployed the survey using Google Forms to our nine team members (CEO, content team, marketing team), giving each of them 15-20 minutes to complete it independently.

3. Facilitation

After the survey, I led a one-hour group workshop to discuss our experiences and findings. This session revealed how the team's internal knowledge had masked usability issues that were glaringly obvious to new users.

What the Team Discovered

Too Many Options on Landing Page

The website presented overwhelming choices without clear hierarchy or guidance.

"Too many options on landing page, I didn't know where to start."

Excessive Clicking Required

Information was buried deep in the site structure, requiring multiple clicks to find basic resources.

"I found helpful info immediately, and with each click there was more. A LOT... didn't know where to click next, but just chose links that stood out for my personal purposes."

Location-Based vs. Topic-Based Navigation

The menu prioritized geographic organization over topical searches, misaligning with how parents actually look for help.

"We have a very location based focus in the top menu, more than topical for our main resources. I'm not likely to come to the site to look for a medium, I'm coming with a topic/question."

Team Recognition of Barriers

The exercise successfully created empathy—team members recognized they had made navigation unnecessarily difficult.

"Wow, do we ever make it hard for people to find what they need!"

Impact

1. Survey Became Ongoing Research Model

The survey was so effective it became the model we used to do research with our Family Feedback Team, establishing a scalable approach to gathering user insights.

2. New Content Strategy Developed

Research findings directly informed a complete overhaul of content development strategy, prioritizing topic-based organization and clearer information architecture.

3. Family Relationships Established

The research process opened channels for ongoing collaboration with families, creating a feedback loop that continues to inform product development.

4. Additional Funding Secured

The research validated the need for user-friendly resources, resulting in additional funding to support continued improvements.

5. Complete Website Redesign

The findings drove a comprehensive website redesign focusing on clear navigation, visual hierarchy, and improved content formatting.

Reflection

This project taught me that sometimes the most powerful research doesn't require elaborate setups or large sample sizes—it requires creating genuine empathy. By having the team experience the website as users would, we generated insights that were immediately actionable and impossible to ignore.

The workshop discussion was particularly valuable. Hearing team members articulate their frustrations in real-time created shared understanding and buy-in for change. When the CEO said "Wow, do we ever make it hard for people," it signaled a fundamental shift in how the organization approached user experience.
